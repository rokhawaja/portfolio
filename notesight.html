NoteSight

AI-Powered Attendance & Classroom Analytics

Role Product Manager & Product Designer
Timeline 2024
Type SaaS · EdTech · AI
Stack ChatGPT · Claude 3.5 · v0 · Replit · Confluence · Figma

Overview

NoteSight is an AI-powered attendance and classroom analytics platform designed to help teachers and school administrators automate attendance tracking and gain visibility into student engagement and performance trends. The platform transforms a tedious manual process into a seamless, data-driven experience that empowers educators to make better decisions.

The Problem

Manual attendance tracking was slow, error-prone, and produced no actionable data. Teachers spent valuable class time on administrative tasks, and administrators had no easy way to see attendance patterns across classes or correlate them with student performance. When a student started falling behind, there was no early warning system in place.

  • 5–10 minutes of class time lost per session on manual attendance
  • No visibility into attendance trends or at-risk students
  • Error-prone paper-based or manual digital systems
  • No correlation between attendance patterns and academic performance

My Role

As both Product Manager and Product Designer, I owned the end-to-end product experience from discovery through delivery.

  • Led product discovery through interviews with teachers, teaching assistants, and administrators
  • Conducted workflow mapping to understand existing attendance and reporting processes
  • Designed end-to-end user flows for taking attendance, viewing dashboards, and flagging at-risk students
  • Created low-fidelity wireframes through high-fidelity interactive prototypes
  • Wrote core product specs and collaborated closely with developers on implementation
  • Ran usability testing sessions and iterated based on educator feedback

Design Process

Research & Discovery

I started by interviewing 12+ educators across different school types to understand their daily workflows. Key insights included: teachers wanted attendance to take less than 30 seconds, administrators needed weekly trend reports without manual data compilation, and everyone wanted a system that worked equally well on desktop and tablet.

User Flows & Information Architecture

I mapped out the primary user journeys — from a teacher opening the app to mark attendance in under 30 seconds, to an administrator reviewing weekly absence reports and identifying students who may need outreach. These flows informed the navigation structure and feature prioritization.

Wireframing & Prototyping

Starting with low-fidelity sketches and moving to interactive Figma prototypes, I designed the core experiences: the quick-capture attendance flow, the teacher dashboard, and the admin analytics view. Each iteration was tested with educators to validate usability.

The Solution

The final product delivers three core experiences designed to work seamlessly across desktop and tablet:

  • AI-assisted attendance capture: Recognition-based or quick checklist flows that reduce attendance to a few taps
  • Real-time dashboards: Attendance trends, at-risk student flags, and classroom-level insights updated automatically
  • Simple, responsive UX: Clean interface that works equally well on desktop for administrators and tablet for classroom use

Impact

Faster Attendance

Reduced time spent on attendance entry from 5–10 minutes to under 30 seconds per class

Better Visibility

Increased visibility into student absentee patterns across classes and time periods

Earlier Intervention

Provided teachers with actionable insights to identify and reach out to at-risk students earlier

Key Learnings

Working on NoteSight reinforced the importance of designing for the real context of use. Teachers need tools that fit into the first 30 seconds of class, not tools that demand a separate workflow. By spending time in classrooms and watching how attendance actually happens, I was able to design flows that felt natural rather than imposed.

I also learned how crucial dashboard UX is when users have limited time to interpret data. Every chart, metric, and flag had to communicate its meaning instantly — because an administrator reviewing reports doesn't have time to figure out what a visualization means.
